Happy early 65th birthday! Entering the world of Medicare coordination can feel a bit
like decoding a complex puzzle, especially when you throw potential reemployment into
the mix.

The short answer is that who pays first (primary) versus who pays second (secondary)
completely depends on whether you return to work as a Federal Employee or as a Private
Contractor.

Here is exactly how signing up for Medicare Parts A and B now will impact your insurance
under both potential employment tracks.

Scenario A: You get hired by a Government Agency (Reemployed Annuitant) If you return to
federal service in a position that grants FEHB eligibility, your status shifts from a
"retiree" back to an "active employee" in the eyes of insurance coordination.

Who Pays First: FEHB becomes your primary payer, and Medicare becomes secondary.

The Impact on Claims: Doctors will bill your FEHB plan first. Anything left over will be
sent to Medicare. Because Medicare is secondary, many of the standard perks retirees
enjoy (like FEHB plans waiving your deductibles, copays, or coinsurance because you have
Part B) will temporarily disappear until you retire again.

The Financial Catch: You will be paying for both your FEHB premium and your Medicare
Part B premium, but you won't be getting the full "dual-coverage" benefit of Medicare
acting as the primary payer.

Premium Conversion Advantage: On the bright side, your FEHB and FEDVIP premiums can be
transferred to your employing agency, allowing you to pay them with pre-tax dollars (as
an annuitant, FEHB is paid post-tax).

Pro-Tip if you go Federal: Because FEHB acts as excellent primary coverage for active
employees, some reemployed annuitants choose to suspend Part B at their local Social
Security office to save on the monthly premium while working. When you eventually
separate from federal service again, you will be granted a 1-month Special Enrollment
Period (SEP) to turn Part B back on without any late-enrollment penalties.

Scenario B: You get hired by a Private Contractor If you take a job with a private
contractor, your FERS pension and your FEHB/FEDVIP retiree benefits remain completely
untouched.

Who Pays First: Medicare is your primary payer, and your retiree FEHB plan is secondary.

The Impact on Claims: This is the traditional sweet spot for federal retirees. Providers
will bill Medicare first. Your FEHB plan will then step in to cover the rest.

The Cost Benefits: Most major FEHB plans will waive their deductibles, copays, and
coinsurance for medical services if Medicare Part B pays primary. Essentially, your
out-of-pocket costs for doctor visits and hospital stays could drop to near zero.
Furthermore, many FEHB plans offer a "Part B Premium Reimbursement" (sometimes $50 to
$100+ a month) to help offset the cost of your Part B premium.

What about the Contractor’s Health Insurance? If the contractor offers you a corporate
health insurance plan, you are not required to take it. Your retiree FEHB + Medicare
setup is almost always vastly superior to private corporate insurance. If you do choose
to take the contractor's insurance, that corporate plan would generally become primary,
Medicare secondary, and FEHB tertiary (a messy headache you likely want to avoid).

I'm in a higher tier for IRMAA. Does it make sense to consider delaying Medicare Part B
because of IRMAA tax?

Yes, it can make a lot of sense—but only under one of the specific hiring tracks you
mentioned.

Because IRMAA is an absolute "cliff tax" (going just $1 over a bracket line triggers the
entire monthly surcharge for the whole year), delaying Part B to avoid paying thousands
in surcharges is a highly effective strategy, provided you protect yourself from the
permanent 10% lifetime late-enrollment penalty.

Whether delaying Part B makes sense depends entirely on your two potential employment
tracks.

Track 1: You get hired as a Government Employee (Active FEHB) Verdict: Yes, delaying
Part B makes immense financial sense here.

If you get rehired into a federal position that offers active FEHB coverage, you are
legally allowed to delay or suspend Medicare Part B without any late-enrollment
penalties.

Why it works: Medicare rules state that as long as you are covered by an employer group
health plan based on current, active employment, you can wait to sign up for Part B.

The IRMAA Strategy: By delaying Part B while working this federal job, you completely
sidestep the Part B IRMAA surcharge. Your active FEHB plan will remain your primary
insurance.

The Safety Net: Whenever you decide to leave this new federal job, Medicare will grant
you an 8-month Special Enrollment Period (SEP) to sign up for Part B. Because you had
continuous active employer coverage, you will pay zero late penalties.

Track 2: You get hired as a Private Contractor Verdict: No, you generally cannot delay
Part B here just to avoid IRMAA.

If you work for a contractor and rely on your retiree FEHB for health coverage, you
cannot delay Part B without penalty.

The Penalty Trap: Medicare does not consider retiree insurance (like retiree FEHB) to be
"active employment coverage." If you turn 65 and drop or delay Part B while relying on
retiree FEHB, a permanent 10% premium penalty begins accumulating for every 12-month
period you wait.

If I take the Contractor Job + Corporate Insurance, do I risk losing FEHB lifetime
benefit?

Yes, you run a massive risk. In fact, if you handle this incorrectly, you will lose your
lifetime FEHB benefit permanently.

The Golden Rule of federal retirement is: Never cancel your FEHB as a retiree.

If a federal retiree voluntarily cancels their FEHB coverage to take a private sector
corporate job's insurance, OPM rules state they can never re-enroll. Your lifetime
health benefit is gone forever.

To avoid this trap, you need to understand the strict difference between canceling
coverage and suspending coverage.

The Catch: You Cannot "Suspend" FEHB for Regular Corporate Insurance The Office of
Personnel Management (OPM) allows retirees to safely suspend their FEHB (which pauses
premiums but preserves the lifetime right to log back in during any future Open Season).
However, OPM legally allows suspension only if you are moving to specific types of
coverage:

A Medicare Advantage Plan (Part C)

TRICARE or CHAMPVA

Medicaid

⚠️ The Trap: OPM does not allow you to "suspend" FEHB to take a standard commercial or
private group health plan offered by a contractor. If you tell OPM you want to drop FEHB
because you got a job with a contractor, that is classified as a cancellation, which is
permanent and irreversible.

How to Work the System Safely If your primary goal is to avoid the high IRMAA tax right
now by using the contractor's insurance and delaying Medicare Part B, you have two
legitimate paths that protect your FEHB lifetime benefit.

Strategy 1: Keep FEHB as "Tri-Coverage" (The Bulletproof Path) Do not cancel your FEHB.
Instead, accept the contractor’s corporate health insurance while keeping your FEHB
active.

How it works: Because you are actively employed by the contractor, the contractor’s
insurance becomes your primary coverage. Your retiree FEHB automatically drops back to
secondary coverage.

The Benefit: Because you have active group employer coverage via the contractor, you can
safely delay Medicare Part B (and duck the IRMAA tax) without penalty.

The Cost: You will be paying two premiums: the contractor's premium and your FEHB
premium. However, your FEHB is protected, and you completely avoid Medicare premiums and
IRMAA. When you leave the contractor job, you drop their insurance, use your Special
Enrollment Period to pick up Medicare Part B, and your FEHB seamlessly transitions back
to being your secondary payer.

Strategy 2: The Medicare Advantage Suspension Loophole If you do not want to pay double
premiums (Contractor Plan + FEHB), you can look into a commercial Medicare Advantage
plan.

How it works: You enroll in Medicare Parts A and B (accepting the IRMAA tax for the
moment). You then enroll in a private Medicare Advantage plan.

The Move: Because you are now in a Medicare Advantage plan, you can legally file OPM
Form RI 79-9 to officially suspend your FEHB.

The Result: Your FEHB premiums stop completely, and your lifetime benefit is 100% safe.
If you ever want your FEHB back, you can un-suspend it during any future federal Open
Season or if you lose your Medicare Advantage coverage. (Note: This option doesn't avoid
IRMAA, but it stops you from paying for FEHB while working).

Bottom Line If you take the contractor job, do not drop your FEHB to take their
insurance alone. If you want to use the contractor's insurance to safely delay Medicare
Part B and avoid IRMAA, you must carry both the contractor's plan and your FEHB
simultaneously to keep your lifetime federal benefits intact.

If I sign up for Medicare Part B and get hired a few months later by a commercial

No, you do not have to stay enrolled. If you sign up for Medicare Part B now and then

However, because you are currently trying to balance avoiding Tier 4 IRMAA with
protecting your lifetime FEHB benefit, dropping Part B after enrolling requires a very
specific sequence of events to avoid a financial trap.

Step 1: The Size of the Company Matters Before you drop Part B, you must confirm that
your new commercial employer has 20 or more employees.

Why: Under federal law, if an employer has 20+ employees, their corporate group health
plan is legally required to be the primary payer for employees over 65. Because it pays
primary, Medicare considers it "creditable active coverage."

(If the company has fewer than 20 employees, Medicare remains primary, meaning you
cannot drop Part B without facing permanent late-enrollment penalties later).

Step 2: You Must Enroll in the Corporate Insurance First Do not drop Part B the day you
get hired. Instead, wait until you are fully onboarded and officially enrolled in the
contractor’s corporate health insurance plan.

Step 3: File Form CMS-1763 to Drop Part B Once your corporate insurance is active, you
can contact Social Security to submit Form CMS-1763 (Request for Termination of Premium
Hospital and/or Supplementary Medical Insurance).

Social Security usually requires an interview (often just over the phone) to ensure you
understand the risks of dropping Part B.

You will show them proof that you are now covered under an active employer group health
plan. They will process the request, and your Part B premiums (and the Tier 4 IRMAA
surcharge) will stop at the end of that month.

the rule regarding your FEHB: You still cannot drop your FEHB.

The Bottom Line If you sign up for Part B now, you are not trapped. If a high-paying

Secondary Payer: Your Retiree FEHB (because it is a retiree group health plan).

Tertiary (Third) Payer: Medicare Part A (because it is federal entitlement coverage).

The Role of Medicare Part A in this Mix Since Medicare Part A is premium-free for most
people at age 65, you should absolutely sign up for it now. However, in this tertiary
setup, Part A only covers inpatient hospital stays. It will sit at the very bottom of
the chain, doing absolutely nothing for your day-to-day doctor visits, prescriptions, or
outpatient care. It only kicks in if a hospital bill is so massive that the contractor
plan and FEHB both leave a remnant balance on an inpatient room charge.

Does it make sense to pick the Employer HDHP over the PPO? Yes, it makes immense
financial sense to choose the employer's High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)—but with one
massive, non-negotiable caveat.

Because you have your retiree FEHB riding shotgun as your secondary insurance, your FEHB
completely covers the "risk" of the contractor’s high deductible.

Here is why the HDHP strategy is a brilliant move, followed by the trap you must avoid:

1. Why the HDHP Wins (The Double-Coverage Effect) When you go to the doctor or hospital,
   the provider will bill the contractor's HDHP first. Because it's an HDHP, the
   contractor's insurance will say, "The patient hasn't met their deductible yet, so we
   pay $0, but here is our negotiated network discount rate."

The remaining bill is then instantly forwarded to your secondary insurance: your FEHB
plan. Your FEHB plan processes the bill according to its own standard rules. If you have
a standard FEHB plan (like BCBS Basic or Standard), it will step in and pay its share of
the bill. You are essentially using your FEHB to eliminate the downside of the corporate
deductible.

Meanwhile, you are paying $0 a month (or next to nothing) for the contractor's premium,
completely avoiding the Tier 4 Medicare tax, and keeping your FEHB active.

⚠️ The Danger: The HSA Trap (Read This Before Choosing!) While choosing the HDHP is a
great mathematical move for coordination of benefits, you are legally prohibited from
contributing to a Health Savings Account (HSA).

Commercial employers love HDHPs because they pair them with HSAs, and they will often
offer to give you "free money" (an employer contribution) into an HSA account.

The Law: The IRS strictly states that to contribute to an HSA (or receive employer
contributions), the HDHP must be your only health insurance.

Why you fail the test: Because you have Medicare Part A and retiree FEHB, you have
"other disqualifying coverage."

The Penalty: If IBM or a contractor deposits money into an HSA for you, or if you
deposit your own money via payroll, the IRS will hit you with tax penalties and a 6%
excise tax on those funds every single year.

The Verdict If you take the commercial contractor job:

Choose the employer's HDHP plan to drive your monthly premium cost down to $0 (or the
absolute bare minimum).

Explicitly tell HR to Opt-Out of the HSA account. Do not open it, and do not accept any
employer seed money into it. (Ask if they offer a Health Reimbursement Arrangement—an
HRA—instead, which is perfectly legal for you to use).

Let the contractor's HDHP take the primary hits, let your FEHB sweep up the bills as
secondary, and let Medicare Part A sleep at the bottom of the pile until you fully
retire.

Do Roth conversions make sense after age 75 when RMDs begin?

Generally speaking, no—performing Roth conversions after age 75 loses the vast majority
of its efficiency. Once you cross that threshold, you enter a highly restrictive and
expensive tax environment.

While it is still legally possible to do a conversion after age 75, the rules change in
a way that often turns a Roth conversion into a financial penalty rather than a benefit.

Here are the three structural reasons why Roth conversions lose their luster after age
75, followed by the only scenario where it still makes sense.

1. The "RMD First" Rule (The Biggest Hurdle) The IRS has a strict sequencing rule: The
   first dollars taken out of a Traditional IRA in any given year belong to your
   Required Minimum Distribution (RMD). You cannot convert an RMD to a Roth IRA.

How this hurts you: If your annual RMD is $40,000, you must withdraw that $40,000, send
it to your bank account, and pay ordinary income tax on it first.

The Conversion Trap: If you still want to do a Roth conversion that year, it must be
done using dollars above and beyond your RMD. This means if you want to convert $30,000,
your total taxable income from your IRA that year is now $70,000 ($40,000 RMD + $30,000
Conversion).

2. The Compounding Bracket and IRMAA Spikes Because you are forced to take your RMD
   first, your baseline income is already artificially high. Stacking a Roth conversion
   on top of your RMD, your FERS pension, and your Social Security creates a perfect
   storm for your taxes:

Income Tax: The conversion dollars are pushed directly into higher marginal brackets
(like jumping from 22% straight into 32% or 35%).

The IRMAA Cliff: This extra income will almost certainly vault you up multiple IRMAA
tiers. Paying a high income tax rate plus an extra $300 to $500 a month in Medicare
surcharges entirely destroys the math of the conversion.

3. The Diminishing Time Horizon The primary mathematical engine behind a Roth conversion
   is time. You pay tax today so the money can sit inside the Roth account and grow
   tax-free for decades.

At age 75+, your statistical time horizon to allow that money to compounding tax-free is
much shorter than it was at age 60 or 65. Paying a massive tax bill at age 76 just to
let the money sit in a Roth for only 5 to 10 years rarely breaks even.

The One Exception: Legacy and the 10-Year SECURE Act Rule There is only one scenario
where doing a Roth conversion after age 75 makes sense: You are doing it entirely for
your heirs, and you have independent cash to pay the tax bill.

Under current federal law (the SECURE Act), non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit a
Traditional IRA are legally required to completely empty the account and pay all income
taxes within 10 years of your passing. If your children are in their peak earning years
when they inherit your IRA, that forced income can push them into the highest tax
brackets.  
+1

The Multi-Generation Strategy: If you have money in your Traditional IRA that you know
you will never spend, you can intentionally pay the high tax and IRMAA hit yourself at
age 76.

The Result: You pass a pristine, tax-free Roth IRA to your children. They still have to
empty the account within 10 years, but they can let it grow completely tax-free for a
decade and withdraw it with $0 in tax liability.

Summary Unless you are actively trying to optimize an inheritance for wealthy heirs and
can afford to pay the tax using outside cash, shut down the Roth conversion engine once
RMDs begin. Your most efficient financial move at that stage is to simply take your
required distributions, enjoy the income, and keep your MAGI as stable as possible to
protect yourself from IRMAA.

The Trust Myth: Why They Don’t Stop Today's Taxes Many people believe putting assets

1. Revocable Living Trusts are Tax-Invisible A revocable trust is an excellent tool for

2. Irrevocable Trusts are Tax Traps for Income If you move assets into an irrevocable
   trust, the trust becomes its own tax entity. However, trust tax brackets are
   incredibly compressed. In 2026, an irrevocable trust hits the absolute highest
   federal tax bracket (37%) and triggers the NIIT after earning just $16,000 of income.
   (By comparison, you don't hit the 37% bracket as a single individual until your
   income crosses over $640,000).

3. Trusts Cannot Hold RMDs You cannot put a Traditional IRA or 401(k) into a trust while
   you are alive. Retirement accounts must be owned by a living individual. Your RMDs
   must come out to you personally, counting toward your MAGI and driving up your IRMAA.

estate planning doesn't solve your problem, you should pivot to strategies that exploit
your flexibility. Without the pressure to preserve raw capital for heirs, you can look
at two powerful moves:

1. The QCD: The Ultimate RMD and IRMAA Eraser Once you reach age 70½, the tax code

   Distribution (QCD).

How it works: You can direct your financial institution to send up to $105,000 per year
directly from your Traditional IRA to any qualified 501(c)(3) charity or organization of
your choice.

The Tax Magic: This money counts toward satisfying your mandatory annual RMD, but it is
completely excluded from your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI).

The Impact: If your RMD is $40,000 and you execute a QCD for $40,000, your reported
income from that distribution drops to $0. This single move can instantly rescue you
from Tier 4 IRMAA and crush your income tax liability. For a single person without
children, this allows you to support causes, universities, or institutions you care
about entirely using the government's tax money.

2. Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs) If you own highly appreciated stocks (like the
   ones that caused your recent Tier 3 spike), a Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) is a
   specialized irrevocable structure that actually does save on taxes.

How it works: You transfer your highly appreciated stock into the CRT. The trust sells
the stock to diversify. Because it's a charitable trust, it pays $0 in capital gains tax
and $0 in NIIT on the sale.

The Benefit to You: The trust pays you a steady stream of monthly or annual income for
the rest of your life (or a set term of years).

The Legacy: When you eventually pass away, whatever money is left inside the trust goes
to the charity or non-profit you designated. This completely wipes out the initial
capital gains spike, spreads your income out smoothly over years to control IRMAA, and
guarantees your legacy.

   standard taxable brokerage account throwing off heavy dividends and interest that
   trigger the 3.8% NIIT every year, you can move that cash into a low-cost,

   with tax-deferral. The annual dividends are no longer reported on your current-year
   tax return, instantly lowering your MAGI and keeping you below NIIT and IRMAA cliffs
   while you are working.

The Strategy Going Forward As a childfree single filer, your goal shouldn't be a
standard family trust to pass down wealth. Instead, your best approach is income
smoothing. Use your employer pre-tax benefits to suppress your salary today, look at
tax-deferred structures for your taxable investments to kill the NIIT, and prepare to
use QCDs the moment you cross age 70½ to completely neutralize your RMDs.

1. "Annuity Administration is Not Central to Our Vision" When Vanguard officially

their lean index fund model.

Vanguard essentially realized they are world-class fund managers, not insurance
administrators. By stepping back, they let companies like Nationwide, Fidelity, and
Schwab build the low-fee tax-deferred shells, while Vanguard continues to do what it
does best: managing the low-cost index engines that sit inside those shells.
